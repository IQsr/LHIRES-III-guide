Why this matters
Every link in this chain can introduce error: pointing offsets at the telescope, slit misalignment at the spectrograph, focus drift at the CCD, or flexure between science and lamp exposures. The rest of this guide is structured around catching those errors before they ruin a night of data. The next section (§2) lets you see the optical path in 3D before you touch the real instrument.

2. Interactive Simulator

Before touching the real instrument, get a feel for how light moves through it. The 3D model below mirrors the LHIRES III optical path — slit, collimator, rotating grating, camera lens, CCD plane. Change the grating density or the micrometer angle and watch where the dispersed spectrum lands on the detector. Two readouts on the simulator make the geometry explicit: the CCD Wavelength Window panel (bottom-left) shows the live λ range falling on the detector, and the What the CCD sees strip (bottom-right) renders the same spectrum the way a real frame would capture it, with Hα, Hβ, Na-D and lamp lines marked when they fall inside the window.

What to try in here
  • Click the Hα preset, then drag the micrometer a few degrees. Watch Hα walk across the “What the CCD sees” strip — and when it falls off the edge, watch the red off-detector warning appear. This is exactly the failure mode behind the case study in §7.
  • With Hα selected, switch between 150 l/mm and 2400 l/mm. The CCD Wavelength Window panel will show how the λ span collapses from thousands of Ångströms to a few hundred — that is the resolution/coverage tradeoff in one screen.
  • Try the Ar 7635 preset at 2400 l/mm: a small micrometer change is enough to push the lamp line off the strip even though the 3D rays still look fine. The grating angle alone does not tell you the wavelength (see §4 and §5).
  • Compare the spectral strip against the lamp-line markers (Hα, Hβ, Na-D, Hg, He I, Ar I). On a real frame these are the patterns you use to verify the wavelength solution — the simulator gives you the “answer key” to build that pattern recognition before the telescope.
A model, not the instrument
The simulator is a geometric teaching aid and the wavelength readout is a teaching approximation, not a physical calibration of any specific LHIRES III unit. It will not reproduce backlash, flexure, focus drift, or the centring offsets that bite real observations. Use it to build intuition, then trust the lamp frame — not the simulator, and not the micrometer — for wavelength.

3. Before Observing Checklist

Run through this every night, in order. Print it out if needed. Skipping items is the single biggest cause of unusable data.

The non-negotiable item
The last item — confirming the wavelength region from an identified lamp line — is the one that has cost the most data in past projects. Do not skip it because “the angle looks right.”

4. How to Tune the Spectrograph

Read this before touching the grating knob

“Do not assume the grating angle corresponds to the intended wavelength region. Always verify the spectral window using an Ar–Ne calibration frame before interpreting science data.”

The LHIRES III uses a rotating grating to select which wavelength range falls on the CCD. The micrometer reading is a guide, not a measurement. Mechanical backlash, hysteresis, the choice of grating (e.g. 600 vs 2400 l/mm), and detector centering all shift the actual window relative to the nominal one.

Build the intuition first
Open the 3D simulator in §2 and sweep the micrometer at 600 l/mm, then again at 2400 l/mm. The same angle change moves the spectrum by very different amounts — that is the geometric reason the procedure below exists.

Procedure

  1. Set the grating angle to the nominal value for your target line (e.g. Hα 6563 Å). Approach the angle from the same direction every time to reduce backlash.
  2. Take an Ar–Ne lamp frame immediately, without moving the grating.
  3. Identify at least two lamp lines on the frame using a reference atlas. Two lines anchor a rough dispersion solution.
  4. Compute the wavelength range covered by the CCD from those two lines. Compare to what you expected.
  5. If the range is wrong, adjust the grating in small steps and repeat. Do not skip ahead to science exposures until the window is confirmed.
  6. Record the final micrometer reading, the lamp frame filename, and the identified lines. This becomes the wavelength reference for the night.
Tip
Take a fresh lamp frame after every meridian flip, after any focus change, and at the end of the night. Flexure is real.

What good and bad focus look like

Focus the spectrograph slit camera on a bright reference star next to the target (Dubhe, magnitude 1.95, was used here). Iterate primary-mirror screw, then the remote fine-focus, until the FWHM is as small as you can make it.

Out of focus
Doughnut. The annulus is the central obstruction of the Schmidt-Cassegrain projected back through the optics. If you see this on a bright star, you are far from focus — coarse-adjust the primary first. Report 6, Fig 26a (Dubhe)
In focus
Tight point. Final focus is reached when the FWHM stops decreasing on the remote control. Use the aperture tool in MaxIm DL to read the FWHM rather than eyeballing it. Report 6, Fig 26b (Dubhe)

5. Wavelength Calibration Guide

  1. Take an Ar–Ne lamp frame at the same grating position as the science frames.
  2. Extract a 1D lamp spectrum along the dispersion axis.
  3. Identify known lamp lines using a reference atlas (start with the brightest, most isolated lines).
  4. Fit pixel position vs. wavelength — usually a low-order polynomial (2nd or 3rd) is enough.
  5. Check the residuals: they should be sub-Angstrom and structureless. Systematic curvature means the order is wrong or you have misidentified a line.
  6. Confirm that your expected science line (Hα, Hβ, etc.) falls inside the calibrated range.
  7. If observing a galaxy or other Doppler-shifted source, apply the known redshift/blueshift and confirm the line still falls inside the frame.

Wavelength atlas — which lines you should see at each screw setting

Picking a screw setting is the same thing as picking a 281 Å slice of the spectrum to land on the CCD. The atlas below collapses all 20 PURS-2025 calibration panels into one wavelength axis: the upper ticks are the brightest Ne (yellow) and Ar (blue) emission lines, and each colored band underneath is the CCD window for a representative micrometer setting. Read it left-to-right and you can answer “at X mm, what should the lamp look like?” in one glance.

How to read this: each band is the ∼281 Å slice of wavelength that lands on the CCD at that micrometer setting (1663 px in 2×2 binning × 0.169 Å/px from the PURS 2025 linear fit λ = 0.1689 · pixel + 4044.4). The four tallest ticks (Ne 5852, Ne 6678, Ar 6965, Ar 7067) are the brightest fingerprint lines — if you can't spot the one(s) inside your highlighted band on the lamp frame, your screw setting is not where you think it is. The 18.35 mm band is the canonical Hα window: confirm by seeing Ne 6402, Ne 6533 inside it and (just past the red edge) the strong Ne 6678.

Common mistake
Mistaking the lamp pattern can shift the interpreted wavelength range by hundreds or thousands of Ångströms. This can lead to searching for Hα in a frame that is actually covering another region entirely — and finding nothing, with no obvious indication that anything is wrong.

How to identify lamp lines without fooling yourself

  • Match the pattern of strong and weak lines, not just one bright line.
  • Cross-check with a published Ar–Ne atlas at your spectrograph's resolution.
  • If two solutions seem plausible, take a second lamp frame at a slightly different grating angle and confirm the lines shift in the predicted direction.
  • Have a second person independently identify the lines and compare results.

What a correctly identified Ar–Ne calibration looks like — vs. one that isn't

Identified · Hα region
Eight Ar–Ne lines matched to NIST. The residuals are sub-Angstrom (RMS 11.32 mÅ) and structureless, so the wavelength solution λ(x)=a₀+a₁x+a₂x² can be trusted across the whole frame. Hα sits comfortably inside the calibrated range. FYP report, Figs 19 & 20 (Phase 1, M81)
Wrong window · ~5100–5400 Å
Frames look fine. Window is wrong. Phase 2 of the M81 campaign sampled ~5100–5400 Å instead of the intended Hα region — redward of Hβ, blueward of Hα, with neither line present. The grating angle was trusted after the Ar–Ne lamp upgrade without re-identifying the line pattern. Lesson: a clean-looking science frame is not evidence that the wavelength axis is right. FYP report, Fig 24 (Phase 2, 2026-03-17)

Always inspect 2D frames before extracting 1D
A 1D plot can hide cosmic rays, sky lines, double traces, saturation, and misidentified targets. Always look at the raw 2D image first. If something looks weird, it is weird.

What a stable spectral trace looks like — vs. a drifted one

Stable trace
Single horizontal trace, Hα in a fixed row. M82 stacked spectrum, 30 min exposure. The red box marks the Hα position the analysis pipeline expects. Guiding was locked on the bright comparison star, so the trace did not walk across the CCD. Report 6, Fig 32a (M82, 04/06/2024)
Guiding drift
Vertical walk across successive frames. Five exposures of Dubhe taken back-to-back during a guiding-stability test; the trace shifts 24.84 pix peak-to-peak over 255 s. Stacking these directly will smear Hα and any narrow features. Re-calibrate PHD2 for the current pier side before resuming. FYP report, Fig 31 (guiding drift test)

Slit camera and 2D spectrum — what they look like together

The slit image and the 2D spectrum share one axis: spatial position along the slit. The right-hand panel is the M81 nuclear region as seen on the slit camera, with the red guide-camera crop marking what the science detector sees. The left-hand panel is the corresponding wavelength vs. spatial-position frame — the nuclear background continuum and the dark arm of the galaxy both appear at the same row on both sides. If a science feature does not line up spatially with something visible on the slit camera, suspect a cosmic ray, a sky line, or a misidentified target before publishing. 7. Case Study: What Went Wrong in This Project

This section is included not to embarrass anyone, but because it is one of the most valuable teaching cases this instrument has produced. Treat it as part of the documentation.

Phase 1 · Oct–Nov 2025
Targeted broad Hβ in M81, a LINER-class low-luminosity AGN. No convincing broad component was detected. Contributing factors: guide-camera instability following an observatory PC reset, intermittent cloud cover, and the intrinsic faintness of broad Hβ in M81 (large Balmer decrement reported by HST).
Phase 2 · Feb–Mar 2026
Switched to Hα on the reasoning that broad Hα is intrinsically stronger than broad Hβ by the Balmer decrement and would give better signal per spectral element. Observations proceeded across multiple nights.
Later
Calibration showed every retained Phase 2 frame had actually sampled ~5100–5400 Å — redward of Hβ, blueward of Hα, with neither line present. The grating angle had been trusted after the upgrade to the wider-spectrum Ar–Ne lamp without independently re-verifying the line pattern against an atlas.
Outcome
The project was reframed as an instrumental feasibility study, delivering a detectability framework, a calibrated noise model from a dedicated 2026-02-13 detector run, an empirical Hα-region noise floor anchored on the 2024 M82 campaign, and a practical warning about wavelength verification — which is exactly what produced this guide.
Lessons that generalise
  • Verify the spectral window with an identified lamp frame before taking science exposures, not after.
  • Inspect raw 2D frames the same night they are taken. Do not defer to “later in the analysis.”
  • Document grating angles, lamp identifications, and decisions in a logbook the same night. Memory is not a calibration source.
  • A null result with a verified setup is publishable. A null result with an unverified setup is just a question mark.

8. Student Quick-Start

First night with LHIRES III: what to do, in order.

  1. Take dark frames matched to your planned science exposure time. NTU's procedure uses darks only — no bias frames, because exposures are always matched.
  2. Set the spectrograph grating angle to the nominal value for your target line.
  3. Take an Ar–Ne lamp frame. Without moving the grating.
  4. Confirm the wavelength range by identifying lamp lines. Do not proceed until this is done.
  5. Point the telescope at the target.
  6. Check that the target is on the slit using the slit/guiding camera.
  7. Start PHD2 autoguiding and confirm stable tracking.
  8. Take a short test exposure (e.g. 60–120 s).
  9. Inspect the trace on the test frame: present, well-centred, not saturated, no obvious cosmic ray pile-up.
  10. Take the long science exposures.
  11. Take a closing lamp frame after each science set, to track flexure.
  12. Take flats using the spectrograph's flat-field unit (from the upgrade kit) at the end of the night.
If you remember nothing else
Steps 3 and 4 (lamp frame + wavelength confirmation) come before any science exposure. Every time. Even on a clear night when you are running late.

9. Troubleshooting

No trace visible on the science frame

  • Is the target actually on the slit? Check the slit camera.
  • Is the exposure long enough for the target's brightness?
  • Is focus correct on the spectrograph collimator?
  • Has the slit width been set correctly (not closed too narrow)?

Guide camera not reliable

  • Is the guide star bright enough? Try a longer guide exposure.
  • Is PHD2 calibrated for the current pier side?
  • Cable snag or dew on the pickoff mirror?

Lamp lines not matching the atlas

  • You are probably looking at the wrong region of the spectrum — this is the dangerous case.
  • Re-identify by pattern, not by single lines.
  • Move the grating slightly and confirm lines shift in the expected direction.

Cosmic rays everywhere

  • Take multiple shorter exposures and combine, instead of one long one.
  • Use L.A.Cosmic or equivalent during reduction.
  • Check CCD temperature is stable.

Saturated lamp frame

  • Reduce lamp exposure time.
  • Saturated lines bleed and shift centroids — do not use them for the wavelength fit.

Wrong wavelength range

  • See §4 and §5. Re-tune and re-verify with a fresh lamp frame.
  • Do not try to “rescue” data taken before the range was confirmed unless you can prove the window from a contemporaneous lamp frame.

Weak continuum

  • Check focus and slit alignment first.
  • Check transparency / cloud.
  • Stack multiple exposures rather than pushing a single exposure to saturation in the bright sky lines.

Target not centred on the slit

  • Re-centre using the slit camera before resuming.
  • Check telescope pointing model; do a fresh sync on a nearby bright star.
  • Check for differential flexure between guide path and science path.
